The dominant Monday night performances of the Steelers and 49ers brought week 1 of the NFL season to a close. It was a great start to the season, and I found it hard to take my eyes off of the TV (and fantasy football app) all weekend.

But in the midst of “participating” in my fantasy leagues this past Sunday, my attention turned to the top players who were NOT on the field: Le’Veon Bell, Tom Brady, and Josh Gordon. All three of these players had been ruled out — with multi-game suspensions — far in advance of week 1.

Despite the guaranteed weeks of inactivity, their “% owned” statistics were still rock solid:

  • Le’Veon Bell —100% owned
  • Tom Brady — 98% owned
  • Josh Gordon — 91% owned

All of these players were early draft picks in my leagues. — but why? They’re out for almost a third of the fantasy season, there’s no certainty of performance when they do return, and you could have selected a guaranteed week 1 starter. Some fantasy experts would tell you that it just doesn’t make statistical sense. The answer: these players have strong brand equity.

Brand equity is a consumer’s perceived value of a product, or in this case, a fantasy football player. Associations with past glory and flashy commercials are enough for people to take a chance, despite these players being handicaps to the team as a whole.

Fantasy team owners believe these inactive players with strong brand equity will generate more points in later weeks of the season, and that this higher, yet delayed point payout is more valuable than drafting a currently active player who will most likely generate fewer fantasy points.

So, whether they knew it or not, most team owners made decisions based on brand equity while drafting this year. Only time will tell if these owners made good decisions, or if they are just suckers for the big name.
